Why Do People Use Crypto?

A clear, honest explanation for anyone who's ever wondered what crypto actually is.

💡 What is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is digital money that exists only on the internet. Unlike the euro or dollar — which are controlled by governments and banks — crypto is managed by a global network of computers following shared rules.

Think of it like email vs. postal mail. Email didn't replace mail instantly — it took time, it had new problems, but it solved things mail couldn't. Crypto is doing something similar to money.

🌍

Global Transfers

Sending money internationally can take days and cost high fees. Crypto can move value across borders in minutes, for a fraction of the cost.

🏦

No Bank Required

1.4 billion adults worldwide have no access to a bank. Crypto gives anyone with a phone access to financial services.

📊

Investment Potential

Some people buy crypto hoping its value increases over time — similar to buying shares. This comes with significant risk of loss.

🛡️

Inflation Hedge

Bitcoin has a fixed supply of 21 million coins. Some use it to protect savings from inflation, since governments can't "print more."

⚡

Programmable Money

Ethereum lets developers build financial apps — loans, insurance, savings — that run automatically without a bank or company controlling them.

🎮

Digital Ownership

Crypto enables true ownership of digital items — art, game items, music rights — that can't be deleted or taken away by any company.

⚠️ The Honest Risks

🎢 Extreme Volatility

Bitcoin has dropped 80% from its peak. Twice. It has also risen 10,000%+. If you invest, be prepared for wild swings in both directions.

🔓 No Safety Net

If you lose your password ("seed phrase"), your crypto is gone forever. There's no "forgot password" button and no bank to call.

🎣 Scams Are Common

The crypto space is full of scams — fake projects, pump-and-dump schemes, and phishing attempts. If it sounds too good to be true, it is.

📜 Regulation Risk

Governments worldwide are still creating rules around crypto. New regulations could affect the value of your holdings.

🧹 Common Myths, Busted

❌ "Crypto is only used for crime"
✅ Less than 1% of crypto transactions are linked to illegal activity. Cash is used far more for crime.
❌ "Bitcoin is anonymous"
✅ Bitcoin transactions are public and permanent on the blockchain. It's actually easier to trace than cash.
❌ "It's too late to get into crypto"
✅ People have said this since Bitcoin was $1, $100, $1,000, and $10,000. Nobody knows the future.
❌ "All crypto is the same"
✅ There are thousands of coins with very different purposes — from digital gold (Bitcoin) to programmable platforms (Ethereum) to meme coins with no utility.

💰 What is Money?

+

Before understanding crypto, you need to understand money itself.

Money is anything that a community agrees has value and can be used to trade. Historically, people used shells, gold, and salt. Today we use government-issued paper (called "fiat" currency).

The key thing about modern money: it only has value because we all agree it does. A €50 note is just a piece of paper. Its value comes from trust in the institution (government/central bank) that issues it.

🎯 Key insight: Money is trust. Crypto replaces institutional trust with mathematical trust.
2

₿ What is Bitcoin?

+

Bitcoin was created in 2009 by a person (or group) using the name Satoshi Nakamoto. Nobody knows who they really are.

Bitcoin is digital money designed to work without any bank, government, or company in control. Transactions are verified by a global network of computers — not a central authority.

Key properties of Bitcoin:

  • Only 21 million will ever exist (scarcity by design)
  • Anyone can send or receive it without permission
  • No single person or government controls it
  • Transactions are public but identities are pseudonymous
🎯 Key insight: Bitcoin is digital gold — scarce, decentralised, and not controlled by anyone.
3

⛓️ What is a Blockchain?

+

A blockchain is the technology that makes crypto work. Think of it as a shared spreadsheet that thousands of computers each hold a copy of.

Every time someone sends crypto, that transaction is added to a "block." That block is then added to the "chain" of all previous transactions — permanently and publicly.

Why this matters: Because thousands of computers all have identical copies, you can't cheat. You can't secretly edit your own copy — the others would immediately reject it.

🎯 Key insight: A blockchain is a shared record book that no one can tamper with — transparency enforced by maths.
4

👛 What is a Crypto Wallet?

+

A crypto wallet doesn't store your coins like a physical wallet stores cash. Your coins live on the blockchain. Your wallet stores the key that proves you own them.

Think of it like a mailbox: your wallet address is the mailbox number (public — anyone can send to it). Your private key is the key to open it (secret — never share this).

Types of wallets:

  • Exchange wallets — easy but the exchange controls your key (like a bank)
  • Software wallets — apps on your phone, more control but internet-connected
  • Hardware wallets — physical USB devices, most secure for large amounts
🎯 Key insight: "Not your keys, not your coins." Anyone who holds your private key owns your crypto.
5

🏪 What are Exchanges?

+

A crypto exchange is a marketplace where you can buy, sell, and trade cryptocurrencies — usually for regular money (euros, dollars).

Centralised exchanges (CEX) like Coinbase, Binance, and Kraken work like traditional stockbrokers. They're easy to use but hold your keys for you.

Decentralised exchanges (DEX) like Uniswap run on blockchain code with no company behind them. You always keep your keys, but they're harder to use.

For beginners: starting with a reputable CEX is sensible. As you learn more, consider moving coins to your own wallet for long-term holding.

🎯 Key insight: Exchanges are doors into crypto. Once inside, consider moving to your own wallet for security.
6

⚖️ Benefits & Risks

+

Genuine benefits: Financial access for the unbanked, fast international transfers, programmable money, censorship resistance, and potential for significant returns.

Real risks: Extreme price volatility, irreversible transactions (no chargebacks), complex technology prone to user error, regulatory uncertainty, and prevalence of scams.

The honest truth: Crypto is genuinely useful technology that is also heavily speculated on. The technology will likely survive. Individual coins may not. Never invest more than you can afford to lose completely.

🎯 Key insight: Treat any crypto investment as high-risk. Diversify. Only use money you don't need.
7

🚫 Common Mistakes to Avoid

+
  • FOMO buying — buying at all-time highs because everyone else is. This is how most people lose money.
  • Ignoring security — reusing passwords, skipping 2FA, sharing seed phrases. This has cost people millions.
  • Trusting influencers — many crypto influencers are paid to promote specific coins. DYOR.
  • Not keeping records — in most countries, crypto gains are taxable. Keep records of every transaction.
  • Chasing cheap coins — a coin at $0.001 is not "cheap." Price per coin means nothing without market cap context.
  • Panic selling — crypto has crashed and recovered multiple times. Selling the bottom locks in a loss.
  • Putting in too much — your first rule: never invest money you need for rent, food, or emergencies.
🎯 Key insight: The most successful crypto investors are boring — they buy slowly, hold patiently, and secure carefully.
